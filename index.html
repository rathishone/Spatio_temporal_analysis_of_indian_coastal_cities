Abstract

Cities worldwide face unprecedented risks from urbanization and climate change. Heat, in particular, is a harmful risk to health, infrastructure, and the economy.

This paper reviews 7 forecasting architectures to find the most accurate model for predicting temperatures. We found the Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM) provides the most reliable predictions, outperforming complex deep learning models and offering a practical tool for urban resilience.

The Challenge

Coastal Indian cities like Chennai, Mumbai, and Kolkata face increasing risk from extreme heat and humidity, threatening public health and economic activity.

Without reliable forecasting, authorities cannot implement timely responses. This research moves beyond reactive frameworks to provide a systematic, proactive prediction tool to anticipate and prepare for heat-related threats.

Conclusion & Future Directions

Key Takeaway

"The main takeaway... is that a solid classical machine model with clear feature engineering ultimately outperformed a more complex, end-to-end style deep learning model."

The LightGBM model was identified as the best and most reliable framework. Its success was not due to complexity, but to intentional and robust feature engineering, which provided clear signals for the model to learn from. This confirms LightGBM as a highly effective and consistent tool for climate forecasting.

Heat Index Formula (U.S. NWS Regression Equation)

HI = -42.379 + 2.04901523*T + 10.14333127*RH - 0.22475541*T*RH - 0.00683783*T*T - 0.05481717*RH*RH + 0.00122874*T*T*RH + 0.00085282*T*RH*RH - 0.00000199*T*T*RH*RH

Key Components of the Formula

The long polynomial equation relates these two variables:

  • HI (Heat Index): The resulting "feels like" temperature (in degrees Fahrenheit for this formula).
  • T (Temperature): The ambient dry-bulb air temperature (in degrees Fahrenheit).
  • RH (Relative Humidity): The relative humidity expressed as a percentage value (between 0 and 100).

The formula calculates a subjective temperature value because it accounts for how high humidity inhibits the body's natural cooling process.
